Prions Are Truly Magic Molecules

by Parth Sharma

The numerous proteins required by cells are not characterised by a singular fixed structure: their structure is often dependent on if and where they are required. When a protein’s shape changes, it is said to fold itself into a new structural conformation.



However, much like cells, proteins aren’t 100% reliable. Certain proteins can assume a ‘misfolded’ structure that does harm instead of good. Known as prions, these baffling yet mischievous proteins cause neurodegenerative diseases that are infectious and fatal.

Prions were discovered through an effort to determine the infectious agent behind transmissible spongiform encephalopathies (TSEs), and are named after ‘proteinaceous infectious particle’. A group of neurodegenerative diseases, TSEs includes mad cow disease (BSE), Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD), and kuru disease.

There are three ways in which disease-causing prions can become rampant: sporadic formation, genetic inheritance, and exposure to external prions. Sporadic formation is highly random and rare - it is a way in which a single protein assumes the infectious structure. Genetic inheritance is exactly what it sounds like - you inherit gene(s) that cause infectious prion formation. Exposure to external prions is when an individual is infected with prions from other infected tissue.

What is worrisome about prions is that their diseases are completely lethal. Once a prion comes into existence, it leads to an exponential chain reaction where other normal proteins are converted into the disease-type by other infectious prions. The body has no means of stopping this like it does for bacteria and virus-caused diseases.

Fortunately, TSEs are extremely rare, infecting about one in a million people annually. So you can rest assured, knowing that you are more likely than not to be safe from this kind of disease.
