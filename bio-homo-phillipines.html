Ancient Homo Species Discovered Near Philippines

by Aryan Dugar on Feb 18, 2019 at 9:48 am

Researchers in Philippines announced, on April 10th, the discovery of a new ancient homo species, homo luzonensis.

Of short stature, much like homo floresiensis, the discovered species was unearthed by a team led by Dr. Armand Mijares from the University of Philippines and Dr. Florent Détroit from the National Museum of Natural History in Paris. The name ‘luzonensis’ was derived from the site of discovery: Callao Cave, Luzon Island.



The research team presented evidence, amounting to 7 teeth, 2 finger bones, 3 feet bones, and a thigh fragment, in a Nature published paper by Détroit et al (2019). The evidence is dated to 50,000-67,000 years ago, and the first clues leading up to this novel discovery were in fact presented in a paper in 2010.

This discovery reflects light on the convoluted evolution of the homo genus, as scientists are questioning how these species - or their ancestors - even reached the island, surrounded with sea from all sides. The earliest evidences of boat-building activities dates to less than 10,000 years ago, whereas stone tools found on Philippines’ islands suggest that the arrival of human species by at least 118,000 years ago.



However, considering the ancient hominid dispersal across this region - in Flores in Philippines, and Luzon and Sulawesi in Indonesia - perhaps migration wasn’t as uncommon as researchers conceived it to be.



What is more peculiar about this species is the ‘mosaic’ mix of traits that their fossils possessed. Some traits, such as the teeth’s small size and simple shape, point to more evolutionarily modern characteristics, while the foot bones resemble those of the Australopithecus, which became extinct two million years ago.



This discovery challenges the traditional straight-sailing view of human evolution, showing that our species’ evolution didn’t smoothly occur from primitive to modern. William Jungers, a palaeoanthropologist in Stony Brook University, New York, remarks, “Island southeast Asia appears to be full of paleontological surprises that complicate simple scenarios of human evolution.”
