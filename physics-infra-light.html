Novel Materials Convert Visible Into Infrared Light

by Chaitanyaa Nair

In the 17th issue of Nature Magazine, scientists from Columbia University, in collaboration with Harvard researchers, published research after successfully developing a chemical process that could convert visible light into infrared light. In the electromagnetic spectrum, the acronym VIBGYOR is used represent the spectra of visible light. Infrared stands for ‘before red’. The newly-developed chemical process allows radiation to pass through living tissue and other materials without the damage caused by exposure to high-intensity light.



The team, which comprises Luis M. Campos, associate professor of chemistry at Columbia, and Daniel M. Congreve of the Rowland Institute at Harvard, conducted a series of experiments using small quantities of a novel compound that, when stimulated by light, can initiate the transfer of electrons between molecules that otherwise would react more slowly or not at all. "With this technology, we were able to fine-tune infrared light to the necessary, longer wavelengths that allowed us to noninvasively pass through a wide range of barriers, such as paper, plastic molds, blood and tissue," Campos said.

They used an approach called triplet fusion upconversion, which involves a chain of processes that basically fuses two infrared photons into a single visible light photon. Photons are particles which can carry light energy. Most technologies only capture visible light, which means that the rest of the solar spectrum goes to waste. This waste light could potentially be used by the newly developed technology to kill or inhibit the growth of cancer cells in places where the current ways of therapy could not reach. The current ways of therapy are also harmful to human beings as they kill healthy cells along with cancer cells and use toxic drugs as helpers. Thus, the use of this ground-breaking technology can help irradiate tumour cells from selectively targeted sites. Not only could this technology cure cancer, but a number of diseases and conditions such as damaged nerves and spinal cords, hearing loss and traumatic brain injuries.
