Ice Repelling Materials

Salil Sodhi on March 18, 2019 at 1:08 pm

It is ironic that global warming led to the freezing of the American Midwest. Major US cities have come to a grinding halt as the temperature dips tens of degrees below freezing temperature. This provides the opportunity to draw the public’s attention to the latest research in ice-repelling materials.



Recent reports suggest that researchers at the University of Houston have created a new material capable of repelling ice from any surface. The silicone polymer (a long chain of molecules formed by silicon) coating can be applied as a spray on nearly any surface.

“We have developed a new physical concept and the corresponding icephobic material shows extremely low ice adhesion while having long-term mechanical, chemical and environmental durability.” said the researchers.



The new material works on the principle of ‘elastic energy localization’. This triggers cracks at places where the ice meets the material, preventing the accumulation of ice on the material’s surface. The new material can be seen in action here:



Testing of the new material revealed that the material is both mechanically durable and uninfluenced by ultraviolet rays. This makes it well suited for aerospace applications where the material can prevent ice buildup on the aircraft’s structure and not be affected by UV exposure at high altitudes. Other potential applications include protecting transmission lines and coating car windshields.



With continuous development, the new material may realize its potential to benefit communities in extremely cold environments.
